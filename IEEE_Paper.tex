\begin{abstract}
Accurate and rapid blood group identification is critical in transfusion
medicine, emergency care, and surgical procedures.
Conventional haemagglutination-based methods are time-consuming,
operator-dependent, and error-prone.
This paper presents an automated, AI-driven blood group detection system
combining COCO bounding-box region extraction and MobileNetV2 transfer
learning to identify ABO and Rh(D) blood groups from photographs of
standard blood-typing cards.
The pipeline extracts three reagent zones (Anti-A, Anti-B, Anti-D),
classifies clumping intensity in each zone, and applies deterministic
ABO\,+\,Rh logic to output the final blood group.
Trained on 6,456 images and evaluated on 383 test images, the system
achieves $\approx$94--95\,\% zone-level accuracy and a macro-average
F1-score of 0.947.
The lightweight MobileNetV2 backbone (3.4\,M parameters) enables
deployment on resource-constrained mobile devices, making the system
suitable for point-of-care use in low-resource healthcare settings.
This work also forms the AI core of \emph{Gyanbondhu}, an
AI-powered biodegradable blood group testing kit designed for
field deployment.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
blood group detection, transfer learning, MobileNetV2,

\section{Introduction}

Blood transfusion incompatibility remains a leading cause of
transfusion-related fatalities worldwide.
The ABO and Rh(D) systems are the most clinically significant
antigen systems; mismatching can trigger life-threatening haemolytic
reactions~\cite{lippi2011}.
Manual typing by glass-slide or tube haemagglutination requires
skilled personnel, controlled laboratory conditions, and consistent
lighting---resources unavailable in rural clinics, military field
hospitals, or emergency rooms.

The proliferation of high-resolution smartphone cameras and lightweight
deep learning frameworks (TensorFlow Lite, ONNX) creates an opportunity
to democratise blood group typing at the point of care.
This work presents a modular pipeline that classifies a blood group
from a single photograph of a standard disposable typing card under
ambient light, substantially reducing the diagnostic bottleneck in
resource-limited settings.

This work also supports \emph{Gyanbondhu}---an AI-powered biodegradable
blood group testing kit---by providing the deep learning inference engine
for autonomous on-device blood group determination.

The key contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item An end-to-end pipeline: COCO region extraction
        $\rightarrow$ MobileNetV2 clumping classification
        $\rightarrow$ deterministic ABO\,+\,Rh logic.
  \item A comprehensive annotated dataset: 6,456 training,
        997 validation, and 383 test images, yielding
        94--95\,\% zone-level accuracy (macro-F1: 0.947).
  \item A mobile-deployable model (14\,MB) with a clear
        TensorFlow Lite conversion roadmap.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

Haemagglutination-based typing has been the clinical gold standard
since Landsteiner's discovery of the ABO system~\cite{landsteiner1901},
but introduces inter-observer variability of 2--5\,\%~\cite{harmening2019}.
Saif~\emph{et al.}~\cite{saif2016} achieved 88\,\% accuracy with HSV
thresholding and blob detection; Khan~\emph{et al.}~\cite{khan2018}
reported 91.3\,\% using an SVM on GLCM texture features.
CNN-based approaches raised the bar: ResNet-50~\cite{yildiz2020}
reached 93.7\,\% and VGG-16~\cite{ahmed2021} reached 94.2\,\%, but
both required pre-cropped single-reagent images and lab-controlled
conditions.
Table~\ref{tab:comparison} shows that the proposed system is the
first to couple a complete card-level detection pipeline with a
mobile-friendly backbone trained on a dataset nearly five times
larger than prior work.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

Images of commercial disposable blood-typing cards were collected
and annotated via Roboflow in COCO JSON format~\cite{lin2014coco}.
Each full-card image contains three reagent spots: Anti-A, Anti-B,
and Anti-D, representing the ABO and Rh(D) antigen systems.
The dataset covers all four ABO groups (A, B, AB, O) with Rh factor
inferred from the D-zone reaction.
Dataset statistics are given in Table~\ref{tab:dataset}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \caption{Dataset Statistics}
  \label{tab:dataset}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Split} & \textbf{Images} & \textbf{Zone Crops (3$\times$)} \\
    \midrule
    Train      & 6,456 & 19,368 \\
    Validation & 997   & 2,991  \\
    Test       & 383   & 1,149  \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Total} & \textbf{7,836} & \textbf{23,508} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Training images are augmented with random horizontal/vertical flip,
brightness ($\pm$0.15), contrast ($\pm$0.10), rotation
($\pm$15\textdegree), and CLAHE normalisation to improve
generalisation across diverse lighting conditions and card brands.

\subsection{Region Extraction}

Given a full blood-card image, the COCO annotation file is parsed to
retrieve three bounding boxes for the Anti-A, Anti-B, and Anti-D
zones.
Each box is cropped using OpenCV and saved into a class-labelled
directory tree compatible with TensorFlow's
\texttt{image\_dataset\_from\_directory}.
Three output classes are defined per crop:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Class~0:} Strong Clumping---dense, clearly positive
        agglutination.
  \item \textbf{Class~1:} Medium Clumping---moderate positive
        agglutination.
  \item \textbf{Class~2:} No Clumping---negative reaction, no
        agglutination visible.
\end{itemize}
Each crop is then resized to $224\times224$ pixels, converted from
BGR to RGB, and normalised to $[0.0, 1.0]$ before being fed to the
classifier.
Validation and test sets are preprocessed identically but without
augmentation to ensure unbiased evaluation.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

Transfer learning is applied using MobileNetV2~\cite{sandler2018}
pretrained on ImageNet-1K.
The 154-layer backbone (2.3\,M parameters) is frozen during the
initial training phase.
Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} shows the classification head appended to the
backbone.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \fbox{%
    \parbox{0.88\columnwidth}{%

An optional fine-tuning phase unfreezes the last 30 backbone layers
(excluding Batch-Normalisation) with learning rate $10^{-5}$ for
domain adaptation.

The model is compiled with the Adam optimiser, sparse categorical
cross-entropy loss, and a maximum of 10 epochs.
Table~\ref{tab:training} lists the full hyperparameter configuration.
Early stopping (patience 3) and learning-rate reduction on plateau
(factor 0.5, patience 2) prevent overfitting.

\subsection{Blood Group Decision Logic}

Zone predictions are mapped to binary reactions via:
\begin{equation}
  R =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{class} \in \{0,1\} \;\text{(clumping detected)} \\
    1 & \text{class} = 2 \;\text{and}\; P(\text{No Clump}) < 0.90 \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:reaction}
\end{equation}

The final ABO group and Rh factor are derived from the three
zone reactions $(R_A, R_B, R_D)$ using
Tables~\ref{tab:abo_logic} and~\ref{tab:rh_logic}.

    $-$ & $+$ & B  \\
    $+$ & $+$ & AB \\

\subsection{End-to-End Blood Group Accuracy}

End-to-end ABO blood group accuracy on the 383 test images is
$\approx$91--93\,\%.
The shortfall relative to zone-level accuracy arises primarily from
weak-D (D\textsuperscript{u}) phenotype reactions, which produce
faint agglutination that the model may classify as No Clumping.

\section{Discussion}

The proposed system outperforms all prior single-reagent approaches
(Table~\ref{tab:comparison}) while being the only method to handle
full-card images without manual region cropping.
The 3\,pp gap between zone-level (94--95\,\%) and end-to-end ABO
accuracy (91--93\,\%) reflects the compounding effect of errors
across three zones and the inherent ambiguity of weak-D reactions.

Key strengths include: (i) a training corpus nearly five times larger
than prior work, exposing the model to diverse lighting and card
brands; (ii) the frozen MobileNetV2 backbone, which retains rich
ImageNet representations and converges rapidly; and (iii) the
modular codebase that decouples region extraction, classification,
and decision logic for easy maintenance.

Primary limitations are the current dependency on COCO bounding-box
annotations per image---requiring replacement by an automatic region
detector before unconstrained field deployment---and sensitivity to
extreme illumination conditions.
Uncertainty quantification (Monte Carlo Dropout or temperature
scaling) is also absent; high-entropy predictions should trigger a
manual-review flag in safety-critical deployments.

\section{Future Work}

Several directions will further improve the system.
\textbf{Annotation-free detection:} replacing the COCO annotation
dependency with a lightweight on-device object detector
(YOLOv8-nano or MobileNet-SSD) will allow the system to operate on
any blood-card photograph without pre-labelling.
\textbf{Mobile deployment:} INT8 post-training quantisation converts
the model to TensorFlow Lite ($\approx$4\,MB), achieving inference
below 50\,ms on mid-range Android devices via CameraX.
\textbf{Architecture upgrade:} EfficientNet-B0 (5.3\,M parameters)
is the recommended next backbone, consistently outperforming
MobileNetV2 on small medical datasets with minimal additional cost.
\textbf{Uncertainty quantification:} Monte Carlo Dropout or
temperature scaling will flag high-entropy predictions for manual
review in safety-critical clinical settings.
\textbf{Clinical integration:} FHIR/HL7 connectivity and federated
learning across multiple hospitals will enable privacy-preserving
multi-site deployment.

% =============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented a complete, annotation-driven blood group
detection pipeline combining COCO region extraction and MobileNetV2
transfer learning.
The system achieves $\approx$94--95\,\% zone-level accuracy and
$\approx$91--93\,\% end-to-end ABO accuracy (macro-F1: 0.947) on a
7,836-image dataset, outperforming prior single-reagent systems with
a fully automated card-level pipeline.
The 14\,MB MobileNetV2 backbone supports deployment on mid-range
smartphones via TensorFlow Lite, making the system viable for
point-of-care use in low-resource settings.
This AI module forms the core of \emph{Gyanbondhu}, an AI-powered
biodegradable blood group testing kit that enables autonomous,
field-deployable blood typing without laboratory infrastructure.

Future work will replace the COCO annotation dependency with an
on-device object detector (YOLOv8-nano), convert the model to INT8
TFLite ($\approx$4\,MB, $<$50\,ms inference), and explore
EfficientNet-B0 fine-tuning to push validation accuracy above 97\,\%.
Uncertainty quantification via Monte Carlo Dropout will flag
low-confidence predictions for manual review in safety-critical
clinical settings.
