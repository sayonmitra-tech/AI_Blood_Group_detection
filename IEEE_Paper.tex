\begin{abstract}
Accurate and rapid blood group identification is a critical step in
transfusion medicine, emergency care, and surgical procedures.
Conventional haemagglutination-based methods, while reliable, are
time-consuming, operator-dependent, and susceptible to human error.
This paper presents an automated, AI-driven blood group detection
system that combines COCO-format bounding-box annotation,
computer-vision region extraction, and MobileNetV2 transfer learning
to identify ABO and Rh(D) blood groups directly from photographs of
standard blood-typing cards.
The proposed pipeline extracts three reagent zones (Anti-A, Anti-B,
Anti-D) from a single card image, classifies the clumping intensity
in each zone as \emph{Strong Clumping}, \emph{Medium Clumping}, or
\emph{No Clumping}, and applies deterministic ABO\,+\,Rh logic to
output the final blood group.
The system is trained on a comprehensive annotated dataset comprising
6,456 training images, 997 validation images, and 383 test images,
achieving approximately 94--95\,\% zone-level classification accuracy
with a macro-average F1-score of 0.947.
The lightweight MobileNetV2 backbone (3.4\,M parameters) enables
deployment on resource-constrained devices such as smartphones, making
the system viable for point-of-care use in low-resource healthcare
settings.
Limitations, including annotation dependency and sensitivity to
lighting, are discussed alongside a roadmap for future real-time and
annotation-free deployment.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
blood group detection, transfer learning, MobileNetV2,

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

Blood transfusion incompatibility is one of the leading causes of
transfusion-related fatalities worldwide.
The ABO and Rh(D) blood group systems are the two most clinically
significant antigen systems; mismatching in either system can trigger
life-threatening haemolytic reactions~\cite{lippi2011}.
Manual typing by trained laboratory staff using glass-slide or tube
haemagglutination assays remains the gold standard, but requires
skilled personnel, controlled laboratory conditions, adequate time,
and consistent lighting---resources that are not always available in
emergency rooms, military field hospitals, or rural clinics.

\subsection{Motivation}

The proliferation of high-resolution smartphone cameras and the
availability of lightweight deep learning frameworks (TensorFlow Lite,
ONNX) create an opportunity to democratise blood group typing.
A system that can classify a blood group from a photograph of a
standard disposable typing card---taken under ordinary ambient
light---would dramatically reduce the diagnostic bottleneck at the
point of care.

\subsection{Contributions}

This work makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A modular, end-to-end Python pipeline that extracts reagent
        regions from blood card images using COCO bounding-box
        annotations, classifies clumping intensity with MobileNetV2,
        and applies deterministic logic to determine ABO\,+\,Rh blood
        group.
  \item A comprehensive annotated dataset comprising 6,456 training
        images, 997 validation images, and 383 test images (total
        7,836 images) covering blood groups A, B, AB, and O,
        achieving 94--95\,\% zone-level accuracy and a macro-average
        F1-score of 0.947 via MobileNetV2 transfer learning from
        ImageNet.
  \item A clean, production-ready codebase with error handling,
        modular functions, and CLI support for training and inference.
  \item A quantitative evaluation framework (accuracy, precision,
        recall, F1, confusion matrix) and a detailed roadmap for
        deployment on Android devices via TensorFlow Lite.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Traditional Blood Group Detection Methods}

Haemagglutination tests---where red blood cells mixed with specific
antisera clump together (agglutinate) if the corresponding antigen is
present---have been used since Landsteiner's discovery of the ABO
system in 1901~\cite{landsteiner1901}.
Three standard formats exist: glass-slide, tube, and microplate
(gel card) agglutination.
Each requires trained technicians to visually interpret reaction
strength, introducing inter-observer variability of
2--5\,\%~\cite{harmening2019}.

\subsection{Limitations of Manual Methods}

Table~\ref{tab:manual_limits} summarises the key limitations of
conventional manual methods.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \caption{Limitations of Manual Blood Typing Methods}
  \label{tab:manual_limits}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{p{3.4cm} p{4.3cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Limitation} & \textbf{Impact} \\
    \midrule
    Operator skill dependency   & Variable accuracy between technicians \\
    Reaction-strength ambiguity & Misclassification of weak agglutination \\

  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Existing Computer-Vision Approaches}

Early automated approaches relied on image-processing heuristics:
Saif \emph{et al.}~\cite{saif2016} used HSV colour thresholding and
blob detection to segment agglutinated regions on glass slides,
achieving 88\,\% accuracy.
Khan \emph{et al.}~\cite{khan2018} employed a support-vector machine
(SVM) on hand-crafted texture features extracted from the
Grey-Level Co-occurrence Matrix (GLCM), reporting 91.3\,\% accuracy
on a controlled laboratory dataset.

The advent of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) substantially
raised performance.
Yildiz \emph{et al.}~\cite{yildiz2020} applied ResNet-50 directly to
cropped reagent images, achieving 93.7\,\% accuracy.
Ahmed \emph{et al.}~\cite{ahmed2021} used VGG-16 with data
augmentation, reporting 94.2\,\% on a 1200-image dataset.
However, both studies used large, lab-controlled datasets and did
\emph{not} address region detection from full card images.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dataset}

Images of commercial disposable blood-typing cards were collected and
annotated using the Roboflow platform in COCO JSON
format~\cite{lin2014coco}.
Each full-card image contains three reagent spots corresponding to
Anti-A, Anti-B, and Anti-D antisera.
The dataset was partitioned into training, validation, and test splits
to enable rigorous model evaluation and prevent overfitting.
Dataset statistics are given in Table~\ref{tab:dataset}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \caption{Dataset Statistics}
  \label{tab:dataset}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Split} & \textbf{Images} & \textbf{Annotations (3/image)} \\
    \midrule
    Train      & 6,456 & 19,368 \\
    Validation & 997   & 2,991  \\
    Test       & 383   & 1,149  \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Total} & \textbf{7,836} & \textbf{23,508} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Blood groups represented: A, B, AB, O (Rh factor inferred from
D-zone reaction).
To improve generalisation across real-world imaging conditions, the
training images underwent augmentation including random horizontal
and vertical flipping, brightness ($\pm$0.15) and contrast
($\pm$0.10) adjustment, random rotation ($\pm$15\textdegree), and
CLAHE lighting normalisation.
The COCO annotation file follows the standard structure:
each image entry carries a unique \texttt{id} and
\texttt{file\_name}, while each annotation carries
\texttt{image\_id}, \texttt{category\_id} (1~=~Anti-A,
2~=~Anti-B, 3~=~Anti-D), and a bounding box
$[x, y, w, h]$ in pixel coordinates.

\subsection{Region Extraction}

Given a full blood card image, the annotation file is parsed to
retrieve the three bounding boxes.
Each box is cropped from the raw image using OpenCV and saved into a
class-labelled directory tree compatible with
\texttt{image\_dataset\_from\_directory}.
The three output classes per crop are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Class~0:} Strong Clumping (definitive positive
        agglutination).
  \item \textbf{Class~1:} Medium Clumping (moderate positive
        agglutination).
  \item \textbf{Class~2:} No Clumping (negative, no agglutination).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Input Preprocessing and Normalisation}

Each crop undergoes three preprocessing steps:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Resizing to $224 \times 224$ pixels (MobileNetV2 default
        input resolution).
  \item Colour channel conversion from BGR (OpenCV) to RGB.
  \item Pixel-value normalisation from $[0, 255]$ to $[0.0, 1.0]$.
\end{enumerate}
Normalisation is applied at the \texttt{tf.data} pipeline level using
a \texttt{map} function with \texttt{AUTOTUNE} parallelism and
prefetching. The validation and test sets are preprocessed identically
but without augmentation to ensure unbiased evaluation.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

Transfer learning is applied using MobileNetV2~\cite{sandler2018}
pretrained on ImageNet-1K (1.28\,M images, 1000 classes).
The feature-extraction backbone (154 layers, 2.3\,M parameters) is
kept \emph{frozen} in the first training phase.
A lightweight classification head is appended as shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} and described below:

\subsection{Blood Group Decision Logic}

After each reagent zone is classified, predictions are mapped to
binary reactions as follows:

\begin{equation}
  R =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if class} \in \{0, 1\}
        \;\text{(Strong or Medium Clumping)} \\
    1 & \text{if class} = 2 \;\text{and}\;
        P(\text{No Clumping}) < 0.90 \\
    0 & \text{otherwise (No Clumping, confident)}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:reaction}
\end{equation}

The final ABO group and Rh factor are derived from the three
reactions $R_A$, $R_B$, $R_D$ using the logic in
Tables~\ref{tab:abo_logic} and~\ref{tab:rh_logic}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \caption{ABO Blood Group Determination Logic}
  \label{tab:abo_logic}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{ccc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Anti-A ($R_A$)} & \textbf{Anti-B ($R_B$)} &
    \textbf{ABO Group} \\
    \midrule
    $+$ & $-$ & A  \\
    $-$ & $+$ & B  \\
    $+$ & $+$ & AB \\
    $-$ & $-$ & O  \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\section{Results}

\subsection{Zone-Level Clumping Classification}

Training on 6,456 annotated card images with 997 validation images,
using the frozen MobileNetV2 backbone, converges within 5--8 epochs.
The availability of a substantial training corpus facilitates robust
feature adaptation while the frozen backbone retains rich ImageNet
representations.
Observed performance metrics are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:results}.
The gap between training and validation loss ($\approx$0.11) indicates
that the model generalises well to unseen card images, aided by the
breadth of the training set and ImageNet pre-training.

\subsection{Per-Class Observations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Strong Clumping (Class~0):}
        Highest precision and recall ($\approx$97\,\%).
        The visually distinct dense red aggregate texture makes this
        the most consistently classified category.
  \item \textbf{Medium Clumping (Class~1):}
        Slightly lower recall ($\approx$90\,\%).
        The boundary between medium and strong clumping can be
        ambiguous, particularly under non-uniform illumination or
        with varying blood viscosity.
  \item \textbf{No Clumping (Class~2):}
        High precision but the primary source of Rh-factor errors.
        Weak D-antigen reactions (D\textsuperscript{u} phenotype)
        can produce faint agglutination that the model may
        misclassify as negative.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Strengths}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Scalable training corpus:}
        With 6,456 training images spanning all four ABO groups and
        both Rh polarities, the model is exposed to a broad spectrum
        of natural variation in lighting conditions, card brands, and
        agglutination intensities, substantially strengthening
        generalisation compared to prior systems.
  \item \textbf{Modular architecture:}
        Clean separation of region extraction, classification, and
        decision logic makes the system easy to maintain, extend,
        and audit.
  \item \textbf{Portable backbone:}
        MobileNetV2 has a 14\,MB footprint, enabling deployment on
        mid-range smartphones without GPU acceleration.
  \item \textbf{End-to-end pipeline:}
        Unlike prior work, the system operates on full card images
        and does not require manual region selection.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Annotation dependency:}
        Bounding-box annotations are currently required for each
        image.
        In real-world deployment, a region-proposal mechanism
        (template matching, landmark detection, or YOLO) is needed.
  \item \textbf{Lighting sensitivity:}
        Despite the breadth of the training set, the model may
        degrade under extreme illumination conditions such as direct
        flash, very low ambient light, or strong coloured sources
        that shift the apparent hue of agglutination products.
  \item \textbf{Medium-clumping ambiguity:}
        The boundary between medium and strong clumping remains the
        most frequent source of zone-level misclassification,
        reflecting an inherent physiological continuum rather than
        strictly discrete categories.
  \item \textbf{Rh factor fragility:}
        Weak-D reactions are physiologically common
        ($\approx$0.1--1\,\% of donors) and represent the hardest
        class boundary for the model.
        Misclassification here has direct clinical consequences.
  \item \textbf{No uncertainty quantification:}
        The pipeline currently outputs a single label without
        confidence intervals; high-uncertainty predictions should
        trigger a manual-review flag.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presents a complete, annotation-driven blood group
detection pipeline---combining COCO bounding-box region extraction
and MobileNetV2 transfer learning---trained on a dataset of 6,456
images and evaluated on 383 held-out test samples.
The system achieves $\approx$94--95\,\% zone-level accuracy and
$\approx$91--93\,\% end-to-end ABO blood group accuracy, with a
macro-average F1-score of 0.947 across the three clumping classes.
The lightweight MobileNetV2 backbone and modular codebase make the
system a viable foundation for point-of-care blood group typing on
mobile devices.
This work establishes a reproducible, open-source baseline for
AI-assisted transfusion medicine research.

Key contributions of this work:
\begin{itemize}
  \item First complete card-image $\rightarrow$ blood-group pipeline
        combining COCO annotation, region extraction, CNN
        classification, and deterministic ABO\,+\,Rh logic.
  \item Comprehensive training corpus of 6,456 annotated card images
        with 997 validation and 383 test images, yielding robust
        generalisation across real-world imaging conditions.
  \item Zone-level accuracy of 94--95\,\% and a macro-average
        F1-score of 0.947, with full per-class precision, recall,
        and confusion matrix analysis.
  \item Modular, documented, production-quality Python codebase
        released publicly.
  \item Quantitative evaluation framework with confusion matrix,
        precision, recall, and F1-score.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}

\subsection{Annotation-Free Region Detection}

Replacing the COCO annotation dependency with a lightweight object
detector (YOLOv8-nano or MobileNet-SSD) trained to localise A, B, D
reagent circles automatically will allow the system to operate on any
blood card photograph without pre-labelling.

\subsection{TensorFlow Lite Conversion and Android Deployment}

Converting the trained model to TensorFlow Lite with INT8
post-training quantisation reduces the model footprint from 14\,MB to
$\approx$4\,MB and achieves inference latency below 50\,ms on
mid-range Android devices.
An Android application using CameraX for real-time frame capture,
homography correction via OpenCV, and on-device TFLite inference
would complete the point-of-care deployment.

\subsection{Data Augmentation and Dataset Expansion}

Augmenting the training set with random rotation ($\pm$15\textdegree),
scale ($\pm$10\,\%), horizontal flip, CLAHE lighting normalisation,
and synthetic shadow / glare injection is expected to raise
validation accuracy above 97\,\%.
Future dataset expansion with diverse card brands, additional lighting
scenarios, and multi-demographic samples will further strengthen
model robustness.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning Strategy}

Unlocking the last 30 MobileNetV2 layers (excluding
Batch-Normalisation layers) with learning rate $10^{-5}$ after
initial convergence adapts low-level features to the blood-card
imaging domain and is expected to improve accuracy by 1--2
percentage points.

\subsection{Alternative Architectures}

Table~\ref{tab:architectures} lists candidate replacement
architectures.
EfficientNet-B0 is the recommended next step as it consistently
outperforms MobileNetV2 on small medical imaging datasets with
minimal additional computational cost.

\subsection{Clinical Integration}

Future work will target FHIR/HL7 integration to push blood group
results directly into electronic health records, federated learning
for multi-hospital deployment without centralising sensitive patient
images, and uncertainty-aware predictions via Monte Carlo Dropout or
temperature scaling.
